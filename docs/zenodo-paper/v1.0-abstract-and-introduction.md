## Abstract

The rapid adoption of AI agent frameworks through late 2025 and early 2026 produced a security crisis whose exemplar is OpenClaw, an open-source framework that as of April 2026 has accumulated 138 Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures across five months of public availability, with Censys reporting over 135,000 internet-facing instances of which approximately 63 percent run without authentication. Independent analysts have converged on the conclusion that the appropriate operator stance on any unpatched deployment is to assume compromise. In parallel, Franklin, Tomašev, Jacobs, Leibo, and Osindero at Google DeepMind have published a systematic taxonomy of the AI agent attack surface, classifying six categories of adversarial content that target different stages of an agent's operational cycle. This paper argues that the failure pattern documented in the OpenClaw record is structural rather than defect-level and that the correct response is architectural. We describe XSOC-NIE-GUARD, a cryptographic mediation architecture that composes device-attested admission, short-time-to-live scoped capability derivation, telemetry-sealed runtime continuity, context provenance anchoring, agent intent envelope enforcement, and fully homomorphic encryption for sensitive context into a five-plane mediation layer. We implement a reference architecture under the Apache 2.0 license that includes a deterministic 25-scenario attack simulation harness and maps each named control to one or more categories in the Franklin et al. taxonomy. Our coverage claim is deliberately bounded. We claim strong structural coverage for four of the six taxonomy categories (Content Injection, Semantic Manipulation, Cognitive State, Behavioural Control), explicitly scope persona hyperstition as a training-time concern outside runtime mediation, and explicitly scope systemic multi-agent threats as requiring ecosystem-level coordination beyond a per-agent architecture. The XSOC proprietary cryptographic primitives underlying the architecture (deterministic symmetric key agreement, post-storage volatile cipher, CKKS-based homomorphic evaluation, telemetry sealing construction) are referenced by interface and by stated security properties only; construction details remain private and are externally validated by the University of Luxembourg, California Polytechnic State University at San Luis Obispo, and George Mason University SENTINEL laboratory. We position this work as a concrete architectural response to the research agenda articulated in the Franklin et al. taxonomy and invite scrutiny of both the coverage claims and the explicit scope boundaries.

**Keywords:** AI agents, cryptographic mediation, homomorphic encryption, capability security, prompt injection, agent attack surface, OpenClaw

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 The AI agent security crisis of early 2026

In November 2025, a single developer released an open-source AI agent framework that rapidly accumulated more than 347,000 stars on GitHub, making it one of the fastest-growing repositories in the platform's history. The framework, originally called Clawdbot and later rebranded under trademark pressure as Moltbot and then OpenClaw, provides a unified gateway that connects messaging platforms including WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack, Discord, iMessage, and Signal to large language model APIs from multiple vendors, along with local shell execution, file system access, browser automation, email, calendar, and a skill marketplace for extending agent capabilities [23, 28]. In February 2026, the project's creator announced he was joining OpenAI to lead personal agent development, and OpenClaw transitioned to an independent OpenAI-sponsored foundation.

By April 2026, the framework had accumulated 138 documented Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures across five months of public life. Seven of those were rated critical, 49 high. Censys data indicates over 135,000 internet-facing instances as of the most recent public reporting [23], of which approximately 63 percent were running without authentication. Two of the critical vulnerabilities warrant individual citation. CVE-2026-33579 permits any client with the lowest permission level ("pairing privileges") to escalate to full administrative control, with CVSS scores ranging from 8.1 to 9.8 depending on the attack vector [26]. The exploit pivots through the victim's browser, defeating the common mitigation of binding the gateway to the loopback interface [28]. CVE-2026-32922 is a separate critical privilege escalation path documented in the same reporting window [27].

The posture recommended by independent analysts is unambiguous. Multiple organizations working in this space, including security research firms and incident response teams, have converged on an assume-compromise recommendation for any OpenClaw instance running pre-patch or without authentication [24, 25, 28]. Palo Alto Networks has published a mapping of OpenClaw failure modes to every category in the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications [28].

The OpenClaw crisis is the motivating example for this paper, but it is not the subject of this paper. The subject is the architectural response.

### 1.2 The failure is structural

The 138 CVEs associated with OpenClaw do not represent a random distribution of implementation defects. They cluster into a small number of structural categories that can each be identified with a specific class of attack on agent systems.

**Credential storage at rest.** OpenClaw stores authentication material for every connected service, including API keys for multiple LLM vendors, WhatsApp credentials, Telegram bot tokens, Discord OAuth tokens, and full conversation memories, in plaintext Markdown and JSON files under a well-known filesystem path. Hudson Rock and other threat researchers have documented commodity infostealer malware families including RedLine, Lumma, Vidar, and Atomic Stealer actively harvesting this file structure [25]. A routine endpoint compromise escalates to compromise of every connected enterprise service.

**Pairing privilege escalation.** CVE-2026-33579 allows any pairing-level client to escalate to administrative control [26]. The exploit defeats loopback binding by pivoting through the victim browser [28]. This is a structural rather than defect-level failure: the architecture permits unauthenticated pairing by design, and the escalation path follows from that design choice rather than from a specific bug.

**Skill marketplace supply chain.** Koi Security audited 2,857 skills in the ClawHub marketplace and found 341, approximately 12 percent, to be malicious. The primary distribution campaign, codenamed ClawHavoc, delivered the Atomic Stealer macOS infostealer to users who installed affected skills [24]. The marketplace architecture loads unsigned community-contributed code into the execution context of an agent that holds broad standing privileges. The twelve percent compromise rate is a natural consequence of that architecture rather than a failure of moderation.

**Messaging channel prompt injection.** WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack, Discord, Signal, and email are all treated as trusted input sources by default [28]. Any content ingested from these channels enters the agent's model context without sanitization, making prompt injection via inbound message a reliable path to arbitrary tool invocation. Shapira et al. [4] report data exfiltration success rates exceeding 80 percent across five web-use agents via task-aligned prompt injection variants of this vector.

**The lethal trifecta.** Willison [3] has articulated a concept widely cited in the current agent security literature: an agent system with concurrent access to private data, exposure to untrusted content, and the ability to communicate externally will eventually exfiltrate the private data under prompt-injection conditions. OpenClaw in default configuration exhibits all three legs of this trifecta simultaneously. The Franklin et al. taxonomy [1] adopts this framing and extends it across six categories of agent traps.

Each of these is a categorical failure of the architecture, not a defect in a specific code path. A patch for any one CVE in a given category does not change the posture; the next CVE in the same category will reproduce the same outcome. This is precisely why expert consensus converges on assume-compromise rather than apply-the-update. Patching a structural vulnerability leaves the structure in place.

### 1.3 The correct response is architectural

The cryptographic security community has developed, over three decades, a set of architectural techniques for mediating high-risk operations with short-lived, narrowly scoped capabilities rather than standing privileges. Related disciplines, particularly the design of systems that process sensitive data in multi-party or regulated contexts, have contributed additional primitives: device-attested endpoints [14], continuity-envelope binding, context provenance anchoring, and homomorphic evaluation for cleartext-free operation on sensitive material [15]. Each of these techniques is deployable today. What has not existed until now is an integrated layer that applies them specifically to the AI agent use case in a way that composes cleanly with existing agent frameworks.

XSOC-NIE-GUARD is that layer. It sits as a single front door between the agent framework and the rest of the world. It does not replace the agent framework. It mediates its access to capabilities, to external content, to sensitive context, and to auditable action. The architecture is framework-agnostic in principle; the initial deployment target is OpenClaw, chosen because the public record identifies OpenClaw as the exemplar case of the class of failure the architecture addresses.

### 1.4 Relationship to the Franklin et al. agent traps taxonomy

Concurrent with the architectural work reported in this paper, Franklin, Tomašev, Jacobs, Leibo, and Osindero at Google DeepMind have published the first comprehensive taxonomy of what they term AI Agent Traps [1]. Their framework classifies six categories of adversarial content that target different stages of an agent's operational cycle: Content Injection, which exploits the gap between human perception and machine parsing; Semantic Manipulation, which corrupts agent reasoning and verification; Cognitive State, which targets long-term memory and learned policies; Behavioural Control, which hijacks agent capabilities to force unauthorised actions; Systemic, which exploits multi-agent dynamics to trigger correlated failure; and Human-in-the-Loop, which exploits cognitive biases to influence the human overseer.

The framing in the remainder of this paper is aligned with the Franklin et al. taxonomy by design. Their paper catalogs the attack surface. This paper describes an architectural response to the portion of that attack surface addressable by per-agent cryptographic mediation. We believe the two works are complementary and that a credible architectural response should engage with the published taxonomy rather than treat it as separate from operator concerns.

Our coverage claim is deliberately bounded and is stated explicitly here so that the reader can evaluate it. We claim strong structural coverage for four of the six categories: Content Injection, Semantic Manipulation, Cognitive State, and Behavioural Control. We explicitly scope persona hyperstition within the Semantic Manipulation category as a training-time concern outside runtime mediation. We explicitly scope the entire Systemic category as requiring ecosystem-level coordination infrastructure beyond a per-agent architecture. We provide partial coverage of the Human-in-the-Loop category, with the honest observation that some defenses in that category are product-design concerns rather than cryptographic concerns. A full mapping from XSOC-NIE-GUARD controls to Franklin categories appears in Appendix D.

### 1.5 Contributions

This paper makes the following contributions.

First, a structural analysis of the OpenClaw security crisis that maps each documented failure category to a class of cryptographic or architectural technique known to address it.

Second, a five-plane mediation architecture (Client Trust, Admission, Action Mediation, Runtime Continuity, Audit and Revocation) that composes these techniques into a deployable layer for AI agent frameworks.

Third, a three-mode fully homomorphic encryption context gate design that, to the best of our knowledge, represents the first commercial deployment of FHE in an AI agent flow, with published benchmarks from our CKKS-based software development kit of 40.28 milliseconds per encrypt operation and 1.29 milliseconds per multiplication operation.

Fourth, an agent intent envelope construction that defeats a class of attack under-addressed in prior work: prompt injection that causes an agent to use a legitimate capability for an illegitimate purpose. The construction binds the agent's declared intent class into the sealed operation envelope, which the adapter verifies before forwarding the operation. A jailbroken agent with a valid capability cannot invoke the capability for a purpose outside its declared intent class without producing a structured audit event.

Fifth, a deterministic 25-scenario attack simulation harness implemented in the reference repository. At the time of publication 17 primary scenarios plus two variants are wired end-to-end against the broker and pass with the expected structured error codes. Each scenario maps to one or more categories in the Franklin et al. taxonomy. The harness is a direct response to the call in Section 6 of the Franklin et al. paper for comprehensive evaluation suites and automated red-teaming methodologies that probe agent vulnerabilities at scale.

Sixth, a compliance framework mapping across NIST AI Risk Management Framework [19], OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications [20], ISO 42001 [21], and European Union AI Act posture [22].

Seventh, an open-source reference implementation at github.com/xsoc-corp/openclaw-nie-guard under the Apache 2.0 license, with a mechanically enforced public and private disclosure boundary that releases the architectural pattern and interface signatures while preserving XSOC proprietary cryptographic primitives behind a commercial engagement layer. The disclosure boundary is enforced in continuous integration by a forbidden-term lint and a private-import verifier.

### 1.7 On the disclosure boundary

This paper describes XSOC proprietary cryptographic primitives by public name, stated security properties, interface signatures, and externally validated performance or correctness claims only. Internal construction details of the deterministic symmetric key agreement primitive (DSKAG), the post-storage volatile cipher (SP-VERSA), the adaptive response cryptography construction (X-ARC), the telemetry sealing construction (TSTL), the XSOC fully homomorphic encryption software development kit parameter selection, and the random number generation subsystem internals are not disclosed. This disclosure posture is enforced mechanically in the reference repository by a continuous integration lint that rejects commits containing forbidden terms and by a private-import verifier that rejects workspace references to production cryptographic packages. External validations of the underlying primitives, referenced throughout the paper, come from the University of Luxembourg Cryptography and Security Research Group under Biryukov and Perrin [16], California Polytechnic State University at San Luis Obispo [17], and the George Mason University SENTINEL laboratory FP5223 audit [18].
